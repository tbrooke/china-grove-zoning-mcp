# Article 14: Judicial Review

## 160D-1401: Declaratory Judgments

Challenges to legislative decisions by governing boards, including validity or constitutionality of development regulations, and actions under G.S. 160D-108(h) or (i) and G.S. 160D-1403.1 may proceed pursuant to Article 26 of Chapter 1 of the General Statutes. The governmental unit making the challenged decision becomes a party to the action. (2019-111, s. 2.4; 2020-3, s. 4.33(a); 2020-25, ss. 44, 51(a), (b), (d).)

## 160D-1402: Appeals in the Nature of Certiorari

**(a) Applicability.** This section addresses appeals of quasi-judicial decisions when certiorari review is required by this Chapter.

**(b) Filing the Petition.** A certiorari appeal begins by filing a petition with superior court. The petition must:

(1) Demonstrate that the petitioner possesses standing to seek review by stating relevant facts.

(2) Provide allegations sufficient to give the court and parties notice of the grounds upon which the petitioner contends that an error was made.

(3) Address impermissible conflicts under G.S. 160D-109 or locally adopted conflict rules affecting impartiality and due process.

(4) Specify the relief requested.

**(c) Standing.** Only petitioners with standing to challenge the decision may file. Those with standing include:

(1) Persons with any of the following interests:

a. Ownership, leasehold, or easement/restriction/covenant interest in the subject property.

b. An option or contract to purchase the property that is the subject of the decision being appealed.

c. Applicants before the decision-making board whose decision is appealed.

(2) Any other person suffering special damages from the decision.

(3) Incorporated or unincorporated associations where members own or lease property in a designated area, or organizations protecting neighborhood interests, provided at least one of the members of the association would have standing as an individual and the association was not created in response to the particular development or issue.

(4) A local government believing the decision improperly grants a variance from or is otherwise inconsistent with the proper interpretation of a development regulation.

**(d) Respondent.** The respondent is the local government whose decision-making board made the decision, except when a local government petitions under subdivision (4) of subsection (c), then the respondent is the decision-making board itself. Non-applicant petitioners must also name the original applicant as respondent. Any petitioner may name property owners or lessees who participated in or applied before the board.

**(e) Writ of Certiorari.** Upon filing, the petitioner presents the petition and proposed writ to the superior court clerk of the county where the matter arose. The writ directs the respondent to prepare and certify to the court the record of proceedings below within a specified date. It also directs petitioner to serve the petition and writ on each respondent under Civil Procedure Rule 4(j), except the chair of a decision-making board receives service. Rule 4(j)(5)d. applies if the chair cannot be found. No summons shall be issued. The clerk issues the writ without respondent notice if properly filed and in proper form. An executed copy is filed with the court.

Upon filing a certiorari petition, a party may request a stay of the board's decision pending superior court review. The court may grant a stay in its discretion with appropriate security conditions. A stay granted in favor of a city or county shall not require a bond or other security.

**(f) Response to the Petition.** The respondent may file a response but need not, except when contending a petitioner lacks standing. That contention must be set forth in a response served on all petitioners at least 30 days prior to the hearing. Failure to meet this deadline may result in continuance allowing petitioner response time.

**(g) Intervention.** Civil Procedure Rule 24 governs intervention motions with these exceptions:

(1) Persons described in subdivision (1) of subsection (c) has standing to intervene and shall be allowed to intervene as a matter of right.

(2) Others seeking intervention as petitioner must demonstrate standing under subdivisions (2) through (4) of subsection (c).

(3) Others seeking intervention as respondent must show they would have had standing to file had the board reached a decision consistent with the petitioner's sought relief.

**(h) The Record.** The record comprises the decision, submitted documents and exhibits, and meeting minutes where the decision was considered. Upon request, it includes audio or videotape of meetings, if recorded. Any party may include a transcript prepared at their cost. Parties may agree to delete unnecessary matters or include additional materials. The record must be bound and paginated for party and court convenience. The local government respondent or respondent board serves a record copy on all petitioners within three days after it is filed with the court.

**(i) Hearing on the Record.** The court reviews all petition issues using the submitted record. The court may supplement the record with affidavits, testimony of witnesses, or documentary or other evidence when the petition raises these issues, with discovery governed by North Carolina Civil Procedure Rules:

(1) Whether petitioner or intervenor has standing.

(2) Whether impermissible conflicts under G.S. 160D-109 or local conflict rules affected the board's impartiality and due process compliance. A failure to object at a hearing by a person with standing shall not constitute a waiver of a right to assert impermissible conflict.

(3) Whether the board erred as set forth in sub-subdivisions a. and b. of subdivision (1) of subsection (j).

**(j) Scope of Review.**

(1) The court ensures petitioner rights were not prejudiced because the board's findings, inferences, conclusions, or decisions were:

a. Violating constitutional provisions, including procedural due process rights.

b. Exceeding statutory authority granted to local government or board by ordinance, including preemption.

c. Inconsistent with procedures specified by statute or ordinance.

d. Affected by other error of law.

e. Unsupported by competent, material, and substantial evidence considering the entire record.

f. Arbitrary or capricious.

(2) For issues in sub-subdivisions a. through d. of subdivision (1), including ordinance interpretation, the court reviews de novo. The court shall consider the interpretation of the decision-making board, but is not bound by that interpretation, and may freely substitute its judgment. Whether the record contains competent, material, and substantial evidence is a reviewable conclusion of law de novo.

(3) "Competent evidence" does not preclude decision-making board reliance on evidence inadmissible under evidence rules if: (i) except for items in sub-subdivisions a., b., and c. that are conclusively incompetent, evidence was admitted without objection or (ii) the evidence appears to be sufficiently trustworthy and was admitted under such circumstances that it was reasonable for the decision-making board to rely upon it. Regardless of timely objection, "competent evidence" excludes lay witness opinion testimony regarding:

a. Whether property use affects other property values.

b. Whether development traffic poses public safety danger.

c. Matters requiring expert testimony under evidence rules.

**(j1) Action Not Rendered Moot by Loss of Property.** Subject to constitutional limitations and case law, an action under this section is not rendered moot if during the pendency of the action, the aggrieved person loses the applicable property interest as a result of the local government action being challenged and exhaustion of an appeal is required for purposes of preserving a claim for damages.

**(k) Decision of the Court.** After reviewing the board under subsection (j), the court may affirm, reverse and remand with instructions, or remand for further proceedings. If not wholly affirming, the court determines what relief petitioners receive:

(1) For procedural error only, the court may remand to correct the error.

(2) If the board failed to make findings such that the court cannot properly function, it may remand with instructions so long as the record contains substantial competent evidence that could support the decision below with appropriate findings of fact. However, findings of fact are not necessary when the record sufficiently reveals the basis for the decision below or when the material facts are undisputed.

(3) If the decision lacks competent, material, and substantial evidence or involves error of law, the court may remand directing appropriate action:

a. When a permit was wrongfully denied due to insufficient evidence or error of law, the court shall remand with instructions that the permit be issued, subject to any conditions expressly consented to by the permit applicant.

b. When a permit was wrongfully issued, the court may remand instructing permit revocation.

c. When a zoning board decision upholding enforcement action lacks substantial evidence or involves error of law, the court shall reverse the decision.

**(l) Effect of Appeal and Ancillary Injunctive Relief.**

(1) If a development approval is appealed, the applicant may commence work while the appeal is pending. However, if the development approval is reversed by a final decision of any court of competent jurisdiction, the applicant shall not be deemed to have gained any vested rights on the basis of actions taken prior to or during the pendency of the appeal.

(2) Upon motion by a proceeding party and under appropriate circumstances, the court may issue injunctive orders requiring action or inaction consistent with the merits decision.

**(l1) Effect of Appeal of Quasi-Judicial Relief.** When a special use permit is issued post-remand from a competent court and no injunction prevents issuance, any appeal of the court's remand order or the subsequently issued special use permit is rendered moot.

**(m) Joinder.** A declaratory judgment under G.S. 160D-1401 or other related civil action may be joined with the petition for writ of certiorari and decided in the same proceeding.

**(n) Stays.** Appeals under this section are stayed as provided in G.S. 160D-405. (2019-111, s. 2.4; 2020-3, s. 4.33(a); 2020-25, ss. 45, 50(b), 51(a), (b), (d); 2021-168, s. 4.)

## 160D-1403: Appeals of Decisions on Subdivision Plats

**(a)** When subdivision regulations provide that plat approval/denial decisions are quasi-judicial, such decisions are subject to review by the superior court by a proceeding in the nature of certiorari. G.S. 160D-406 and this section apply to those appeals.

**(b)** When regulations provide that plat decisions are administrative, the following applies:

(1) Decisions by governing or planning boards may be reviewed by filing an action in superior court seeking appropriate declaratory or equitable relief within 30 days from receipt of the written notice of the decision.

(2) Decisions by staff or staff committee follow G.S. 160D-405 appeal procedures.

**(c)** A subdivision regulation authorizes quasi-judicial decisions if the decision-making entity under G.S. 160D-803(c) is authorized to decide whether to approve or deny the plat based not only upon whether the application complies with the specific requirements set forth in the regulation but also on whether the application complies with one or more generally stated standards requiring a discretionary decision. (2019-111, s. 2.4; 2020-3, s. 4.33(a); 2020-25, s. 51(a), (b), (d); 2022-62, s. 59(c).)

## 160D-1403.1: Civil Action for Declaratory Relief, Injunctive Relief, Other Remedies; Joinder of Complaint and Petition for Writ of Certiorari in Certain Cases

**(a) Civil Action.** Except as otherwise provided for interpretation claims, instead of remedies under G.S. 160D-405 or G.S. 160D-108(h), persons with standing as defined in subsection (b) may bring original civil actions seeking declaratory relief, injunctive relief, damages, or any other remedies provided by law or equity, in superior court or federal court for these claims:

(1) The ordinance, either on its face or as applied, is unconstitutional.

(2) The ordinance, either on its face or as applied, is ultra vires, preempted, or otherwise in excess of statutory authority.

(3) The ordinance, either on its face or as applied, constitutes a taking of property.

When challenging an administrative official's decision enforcing a regulation, parties must first bring ordinance interpretation claims to the board of adjustment under G.S. 160D-405. An adverse board ruling may then be challenged in a civil action under this subsection, with the court hearing matters de novo.

**(b) Standing.** The following provide standing:

(1) Persons with ownership, leasehold, easement interests, or options/contracts to purchase property subject to a final and binding decision made by an administrative official charged with applying or enforcing a land development regulation.

(2) Development permit applicants before the decision-making board whose decision is challenged.

(3) Development permit applicants aggrieved by a final and binding decision of an administrative official charged with applying or enforcing a land development regulation.

**(c) Time for Commencement of Action.** Actions must be commenced within one year after the date on which written notice of the final decision is delivered to the aggrieved party by personal delivery, email, or by first-class mail.

**(d) Joinder.** Original civil actions may be joined with a petition for writ of certiorari and decided in the same proceedings. Civil Procedure Rules govern civil action parties. Certiorari record supplementation by civil action discovery is not allowed unless permitted under G.S. 160D-1402(i). Civil action claims receive de novo review while certiorari petitions use the standard established in G.S. 160D-1402(j).

**(e) Action Not Rendered Moot by Loss of Property.** Subject to constitutional limitations, an action is not rendered moot if during the pendency of the action, the aggrieved person loses the applicable property interest as a result of the local government action being challenged and exhaustion of an appeal is required for purposes of preserving a claim for damages.

**(f) Stays.** Appeals are stayed as provided in G.S. 160D-405.

**(g) Definitions.** G.S. 143-755 definitions apply. (2020-25, ss. 46, 50(b); 2025-25, s. 29(1).)

## 160D-1403.2: No Estoppel Effect When Challenging Development Conditions

A local government may not assert before a board of adjustment or in any civil action the defense of estoppel as a result of actions by the landowner or permit applicant to proceed with development authorized by a development permit when the applicant challenges non-consented conditions. (2020-25, ss. 47, 50(b).)

## 160D-1404: Other Civil Actions

Except as expressly stated, this Article does not limit the availability of civil actions otherwise authorized by law or alter the times in which they may be brought. (2019-111, s. 2.4; 2020-3, s. 4.33(a); 2020-25, s. 51(a), (b), (d).)

## 160D-1405: Statutes of Limitation

**(a) Zoning Map Adoption or Amendments.** A cause of action regarding zoning map adoption/amendment validity accrues upon adoption of the ordinance and shall be brought within 60 days as provided in G.S. 1-54.1.

**(b) Text Adoption or Amendment.** Except as provided in subsection (a), actions challenging development regulation validity shall be brought within one year of the accrual of the action as provided in G.S. 1-54(10). The action accrues when the party bringing the action first has standing to challenge the ordinance. Challenges based on adoption process defects shall be brought within three years after the adoption of the ordinance.

**(c) Enforcement Defense.** Nothing bars parties in enforcement or G.S. 160D-1403.1 actions from raising ordinance enforceability or invalidity claims or defenses. Nothing bars parties filing timely administrative appeals from raising ordinance invalidity as defense to orders. A party in an enforcement action or appeal shall not assert the invalidity of the ordinance on the basis of an alleged defect in the adoption process unless the defense is formally raised within three years.

**(c1) Termination of Grandfathered Status.** When a use violating a zoning or unified development ordinance existed before adoption and was grandfathered but subsequently terminates, a local government shall bring an enforcement action within 10 years of the date of the termination of the grandfathered status, unless the violation poses an imminent hazard.

**(d) Quasi-Judicial Decisions.** Unless otherwise specified, a petition for review of a quasi-judicial decision shall be filed with the clerk of superior court by the later of 30 days after the decision is effective or after a written copy of it is given in accordance with G.S. 160D-406(j). When first-class mail is used to deliver notice, three days shall be added.

**(e) Others.** Except as provided by this section, the statutes of limitations are as provided in Subchapter II of Chapter 1 of the General Statutes. (2019-111, s. 2.4; 2020-3, s. 4.33(a); 2020-25, ss. 48, 50(b), 51(a), (b), (d); 2022-62, s. 54(b).)
